# What Does "Approved by Classification Societies" Actually Mean for Maritime Software?

If you have evaluated maritime software, you have probably seen vendors claim recognition or approval from classification societies. It sounds significant. But what does it actually mean — and why does it matter for how you manage compliance?

This piece explains what classification society approval means for a software system, how it is different from a general marketing claim, and why it matters if ISM compliance and Class surveys are part of your operational reality.

---

## What Classification Societies Do

Classification societies set the technical standards for the design, construction, and ongoing maintenance of ships. Their rules cover hull integrity, machinery, equipment, and operational safety systems. Periodic surveys confirm that a vessel continues to meet those standards throughout its service life.

The major classification societies — ABS, DNV, Lloyd's Register, Bureau Veritas, ClassNK, RINA, CCS, and others — each operate their own rulebooks, their own survey regimes, and their own certification processes. A vessel classed with DNV operates under DNV's rules and is surveyed by DNV surveyors. The same vessel may also hold Flag State certification and face Port State Control inspections, each governed by a different authority.

For a vessel operator, Class is not optional. A vessel that loses its Class certificate — through a failed survey or unresolved deficiencies — cannot trade. Class standing affects insurance, charterer acceptance, and financing. It is one of the most consequential compliance obligations a shipowner manages.

---

## What "Approved by Classification Societies" Means for Software

When a maritime software system is described as approved or recognised by a classification society, it means the system's workflows and documentation capabilities have been reviewed against that society's standards — and found to meet the requirements for demonstrating compliance.

This is not a badge handed out to any software vendor that asks for one. It requires formal review of how the system works: whether it supports the survey planning process, whether it generates the documentation a surveyor needs to review, whether the maintenance and compliance workflows are aligned to Class requirements.

For a Planned Maintenance System or an HSEQ platform, Class recognition means the system has been assessed as suitable for use as evidence of compliance with Class survey requirements. When a surveyor comes aboard and asks to see maintenance records, inspection documentation, or corrective action logs, a Class-recognised system produces evidence in a format the surveyor understands and accepts.

For a generic tool — a spreadsheet, a non-maritime ERP, or a compliance platform built for a different industry — that recognition does not exist. The documentation it produces may or may not satisfy a surveyor. The operator carries the risk of that uncertainty.

---

## Why It Matters Operationally

**Survey preparation becomes structured rather than improvised.** A Class-recognised system is built around survey cycles — due dates, required documentation, condition records. The surveyor's workflow and the operator's workflow are aligned. Preparation for a Class renewal survey is a managed process, not a search through records.

**Documentation is produced in the right format.** Classification societies have specific requirements for how maintenance records, deficiency reports, and corrective actions should be documented. A system built to Class standards produces records that meet those requirements by design. A generic system may require significant reformatting or manual supplementation.

**Deficiencies are tracked to closure in a way that satisfies surveyors.** When a surveyor raises a deficiency, it needs to be formally tracked, actioned, and verified as closed. A Class-recognised system has this workflow built in. The audit trail is complete and structured. A non-recognised system may handle the closure, but the evidence standard may not hold under scrutiny.

**The software itself is not a liability.** If a vessel is detained or a survey fails in part because the software used to manage maintenance and compliance did not meet Class standards, the operator carries that consequence. Using a Class-recognised system removes one layer of that risk.

## Recognition Is Not a One-Time Event

It is worth noting that classification society recognition for a software system is not a permanent certificate issued once and held indefinitely. It requires ongoing alignment between the system's capabilities and the society's evolving standards.

As Class rules are updated, as survey requirements change, and as new regulatory frameworks are introduced, a recognised system must keep pace. This ongoing alignment is part of what classification society recognition signals — that the software vendor has a sustained, active relationship with the societies whose standards they support.

For fleet operators evaluating maritime software, the question to ask is not just "are you recognised by classification societies?" but "which ones, and is that recognition current?"
