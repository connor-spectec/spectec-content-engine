# What Is the ISM Code — and What Does It Actually Require?

If you work in maritime operations, the ISM Code is not a theoretical framework. It is the foundation of how your safety management system is structured, how your fleet is certified, and how inspectors assess whether your vessels are fit to trade.

Yet for many people working in and around compliance, the ISM Code remains something they know exists rather than something they fully understand. This piece covers what it actually requires — and what it means for how you manage compliance day to day.

---

## What the ISM Code Is

The International Safety Management Code is a mandatory international standard for the safe management and operation of ships, and for pollution prevention. It was adopted by the International Maritime Organization (IMO) and came into force under SOLAS Chapter IX.

The core principle is straightforward: every shipping company must have a documented Safety Management System, and both the company and each vessel must be certified against it.

That certification takes two forms:

- **Document of Compliance (DOC)** — issued to the shipping company. Confirms the company's SMS meets ISM requirements.
- **Safety Management Certificate (SMC)** — issued to each individual vessel. Confirms the ship is operating in accordance with the company's approved SMS.

Both must be kept current. A lapsed DOC or SMC is not a minor administrative issue — it affects the vessel's ability to trade and creates immediate exposure in any Port State Control inspection.

---

## What the ISM Code Actually Requires

The ISM Code sets out twelve elements that a Safety Management System must address. In practice, these translate into specific operational obligations:

**Documented procedures for every safety-critical activity.** The SMS must cover how the vessel handles navigation, cargo operations, engine room management, emergency response, maintenance, and more. These procedures must be written down, kept current, and accessible to the crew.

**A non-conformance reporting system.** Any departure from the SMS — a procedure not followed, an unsafe condition observed, an incident or near-miss — must be recorded as a non-conformance. The ISM Code requires that NCRs are investigated, root causes identified, and corrective actions assigned and verified as closed. This is not optional, and it is not a paper exercise. Auditors look specifically at the quality of NCR tracking and corrective action closure.

**Internal audits.** The company must conduct regular internal audits of both the shore-based management system and each vessel. The purpose is to identify non-conformances before an external inspector does and to demonstrate a culture of continuous improvement.

**Safety observations and near-miss reporting.** Crew must have a mechanism to report hazards and near-misses without fear of blame. The SMS must show these are captured, reviewed, and acted on.

**Drill records and emergency preparedness.** Muster drills, fire drills, emergency steering drills, and lifeboat drills must be documented. Frequency and participation records must be available for inspection.

**Management review.** Senior management must formally review the performance of the SMS at defined intervals, demonstrating that the system is actively managed — not simply maintained on paper.

## Why the ISM Code Is Hard to Manage Without a System

Each of these requirements generates documentation. NCRs, corrective actions, drill records, audit reports, procedure updates, certificate records — across multiple vessels, managed by crews that rotate every few months.

The challenge is not understanding what the ISM Code requires. Most experienced maritime professionals know the framework. The challenge is executing it consistently, at scale, with a traceable record that satisfies an auditor who has seen every creative interpretation of "documented and closed."

When compliance records live in spreadsheets, shared drives, and email chains — vessel by vessel, rotation by rotation — the ISM Code's requirements are met in spirit more often than they are met in evidence. An auditor does not give credit for effort. They look at what the record shows.

A centralised system changes this. When NCRs are logged, tracked, and closed in one place — with timestamps, accountability, and a full audit trail — the documentation is not assembled before an inspection. It is already there.

## What ISM Auditors Look For

When a Class surveyor or Flag State auditor reviews your SMS compliance, they are not checking whether your procedures exist. They are checking whether they are followed.

The questions that create the most exposure are:

- Are NCRs being raised, or is the system clean because problems are not being recorded?
- When corrective actions are assigned, are they actually verified as closed — or just marked complete?
- Are internal audits identifying genuine findings, or are they producing blank reports?
- Is the crew familiar with the procedures — or do they exist only in a binder no one reads?

The quality of your ISM compliance is visible in the data. A well-managed SMS produces a steady flow of observations, NCRs, and closed corrective actions. A poorly managed one produces silence — which is itself a finding.

## The Connection Between ISM and Your Wider HSEQ Framework

The ISM Code sits at the foundation of maritime HSEQ compliance, but it does not stand alone. Class surveys, Flag State inspections, and Port State Control all use the SMS as a reference point. An NCR that is raised internally and closed before a PSC inspection is a sign of a healthy system. The same finding raised by a PSC inspector — because it was never recorded internally — is a deficiency, and potentially a detention.

This is why ISM compliance is most effective when it is embedded in daily operations rather than managed as a separate administrative discipline.
